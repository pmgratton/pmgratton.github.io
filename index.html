Quantitative reporting

Data Journalism

Working directly with federal microdata to produce original findings — not just citing other outlets' analysis, but generating it.

Most financial journalism uses data secondhand — citing a Fed report, quoting a think tank's findings, linking to someone else's chart. A different approach starts from the source material itself: pulling raw microdata, applying appropriate weights and methodology, and generating findings that haven't been published elsewhere.

That means working directly with the Census Bureau's American Community Survey Public Use Microdata Sample (ACS PUMS), the Federal Reserve's Survey of Consumer Finances (SCF), the Bureau of Labor Statistics Consumer Expenditure Survey, and ADP's national employment datasets. Each requires different handling — the SCF's five-imputation structure demands Rubin's rules for correct standard errors; the PUMS requires person and household weights plus replicate weights for variance estimation. The output, when done correctly, is findings you can defend to a statistician and explain to anyone.

Over a decade at Investopedia, this work produced the framework for a comparison story franchise that benchmarks readers' financial situations against demographically similar peers — a content architecture built entirely on original data analysis rather than recycled findings. The "house-rich, cash-poor" analysis, the generational 401(k) benchmarks, the state-level housing burden maps — these start from raw federal data files, not from other publications.

Alongside this quantitative work sits a parallel practice of academic research translation: reading primary studies, evaluating methodology, and making findings legible for general audiences without losing their substance. The 2003 hiring discrimination piece, for instance, synthesized a meta-analysis of 90+ field experiments spanning two decades — the work was not finding a study and summarizing it, but evaluating a body of evidence and explaining what it collectively shows.

The data visualization side — 1,500+ original Datawrapper charts produced at Investopedia — is less about technical complexity than about editorial judgment: which chart type serves the story, how axes should be framed, when a table beats a graphic. The volume reflects sustained daily practice; the craft is in the decisions that make numbers intelligible rather than merely present.

Work in progress

Grift Society

How speculation and confidence became the organizing principles of contemporary American capitalism.

Something changed in American capitalism in the last four decades that isn't fully captured by the standard accounts of inequality, financialization, or the rise of the precariat. The change is this: the confidence game moved to the center of economic life.

Grift Society argues that the United States has undergone a structural transformation in which speculation, hype, and extractive financial products have ceased to be aberrations of the market and have become its characteristic features. The 401(k) replaced the pension and made every worker a retail investor exposed to market risk. Robinhood gamified trading for a generation raised on social media feedback loops. Sports betting was legalized and scaled nationally with little public deliberation. Prediction markets — once peripheral — now set prices on elections and policy outcomes. The throughline isn't a conspiracy. It's a logic: risk was privatized, asymmetric information was monetized, and the distinction between investment and speculation was systematically blurred.

The book tracks this transformation through specific cases and actors rather than abstract forces. It uses the career of Howard Lutnick — from Cantor Fitzgerald's post-9/11 survival to his role as a Bitcoin evangelist to his position in the Trump administration — as a recurring figure who embodies the continuities between Wall Street's financial engineering and the crypto speculation economy. The game didn't change; the instruments did.

The theoretical intervention the book makes is to propose homo speculans as the successor figure to the neoliberal homo economicus: where the rational actor of economic theory made calculated investments in human capital, the speculative subject bets on outcomes they cannot control, sustained by the ideological conviction that the market rewards the bold and punishes only the unlucky. This is not just an economic category but a moral one — it shapes how people understand financial failure, who they blame, and what remedies they find credible.

Background

About

I'm an editor and writer who covers the intersection of economic power, financial markets, and everyday life — the places where a Fed decision or a tariff schedule or a corporate diversity rollback lands on real people with real bills.

For the past decade I've worked at Investopedia, one of the web's largest personal finance destinations, most recently as Senior Editor. In that role I've directed day-to-day editorial workflow for a ten-person team, produced and edited hundreds of articles annually, built more than 1,500 original data visualizations, and helped lead the publication's strategic response to the post-SEO content landscape as a member of the Google Discover optimization taskforce. I've also maintained an unusually active writing output for a senior editor — three to five bylined pieces per week, covering breaking news, data investigations, policy analysis, and narrative features simultaneously.

My background is unusual for financial journalism. Before moving into editorial work full-time, I trained as a philosopher — my PhD focused on political theory and continental thought, and I held faculty positions at several universities over nearly two decades. That formation shapes how I think about economic stories: I'm drawn to the structural over the symptomatic, the historical over the immediately reactive, the question behind the question. It's why a story about 401(k)s becomes an inquiry into how ordinary Americans were quietly conscripted into financial speculation. It's why a story about financial advisors and diversity became a long-form investigation into decades of industry failure rather than an opinion survey.

I'm currently at work on Grift Society, a book examining how speculation and confidence games became the organizing principles of contemporary American capitalism — from 401(k)s and Robinhood to prediction markets and crypto.
