Writing

I'm drawn to the structural question behind the news hook, the historical precedent behind the current crisis, the body of research that goes deeper than the single study everyone is citing. That orientation shapes two decades of writing about money, power, and the economy—across financial and general interest publications. The work ranges from breaking news and daily market commentary to long-form data analyses and features, to investigative, policy, and accountability reporting. Alongside that, I write long-form essays and criticism for publications including Liberal Currents, the Oxford Literary Review, and the Los Angeles Review of Books—work that draws on my background in political philosophy and intellectual history.

Quantitative reporting

Data Journalism

Original data reporting built from source—pulling, processing, and visualizing data across markets, government surveys, and academic research to find stories that aren't in anyone else's spreadsheet.

Most financial journalism is passive about data—citing a Fed report, pulling a number from a think tank release, linking to a chart someone else built. The alternative is to start from the source: pulling raw datasets, writing the code to process them, and generating findings that haven't been published anywhere else. That's the approach here, whether the story calls for Census microdata, FRED time series, capital markets data, IMF figures, or housing indices.

On the survey microdata side, that means working with datasets like the Census Bureau's ACS PUMS (housing, income, demographics at the person and household level), the Federal Reserve's Survey of Consumer Finances (wealth, debt, and retirement savings), the BLS Consumer Expenditure Survey, the Current Population Survey, and ADP's employment data—among others. Each has its own structure, weighting scheme, and pitfalls. On the markets side, it means building and maintaining data feeds for equities, commodities, currencies, and fixed income—whatever the story requires on a given day.

At Investopedia, this work produced the framework for a comparison story franchise that benchmarks readers' financial situations against demographically similar peers. The approach was deliberately different from how competitors handle data: rather than waiting for a pre-packaged report from an institute or reposting another outlet's analysis, the stories here start from raw datasets and produce findings tailored to the specific question. The "house-rich, cash-poor" analysis, the generational 401(k) benchmarks, the state-level housing burden maps—all built from source.

Alongside quantitative work sits a parallel practice of academic research translation: reading primary studies, evaluating methodology, and making findings accessible without losing their substance. The hiring discrimination piece, for instance, synthesized a meta-analysis of 90+ field experiments spanning two decades—not finding a study and summarizing it, but evaluating a two-decade-long set of evidence and explaining what it means for the reader.

The data visualization side—1,500+ original charts—is less about technical complexity than editorial judgment: which chart type serves the story, how axes should be framed, and when a table beats a graphic.

Grift Society

Something changed in American capitalism in the last four decades that isn't fully captured by the standard accounts of inequality, financialization, or the rise of the precariat. The change is this: neoliberalism has not simply failed—it has metastasized into the grift society.

Grift Society argues that the United States has undergone a structural transformation in which speculation, hype, and extractive financial products have ceased to be aberrations of the market and have become its characteristic features. The 401(k) replaced the pension and made every worker a retail investor exposed to market risk. Robinhood gamified trading for a generation raised on social media feedback loops. Sports betting was legalized and scaled nationally with little public deliberation. Prediction markets—once peripheral—now set prices on elections and policy outcomes. Risk was privatized, asymmetric information was monetized, and the distinction between investment and speculation was systematically blurred.

What has emerged is something at once more transparent and more predatory than the neoliberal order it replaced: a system organized not around production, innovation, or even the pretense of shared prosperity, but around the logic of the confidence game. The book tracks its argument through specific cases, using the career of Howard Lutnick—from Cantor Fitzgerald's post-9/11 survival to his role as a Bitcoin evangelist to his position in the Trump administration—as a recurring throughline connecting Wall Street's financial engineering to the crypto speculation economy.

The theoretical intervention the book makes is to propose homo speculans as the successor figure to the neoliberal homo economicus: where the rational actor of economic theory made calculated investments in human capital, the speculative subject bets on outcomes they cannot control, sustained by the ideological conviction that the market rewards the bold and punishes only the unlucky. This is not just an economic category but a moral one—it shapes how people understand financial failure, who they blame, and what remedies they find credible.

  • Intro

    The Confidence Economy

    Opens with a triptych of financial spectacles from a single news cycle—Newsmax briefly surpassing Warner Bros. Discovery in market cap, the NYSE Texas exchange inaugurating with Trump Media warrants, Trump pardoning a gallery of crypto fraudsters—and establishes the book's central claim: we have moved from neoliberalism to the grift society, a phase of capitalism in which what matters is not whether something is true, useful, or productive, but whether it can generate enough belief to circulate.

  • Ch. 1

    From Neoliberalism to the Grift Society

    Traces the historical arc from neoliberalism to the grift economy—arguing that the latter is not a betrayal of neoliberal logic but its grotesque perfection. The centerpiece is shareholder value maximization as a case study: from Friedman's 1970 essay through the explosion of stock options and corporate short-termism to Delaware's rewriting of corporate law to protect executives from their own shareholders.

  • Ch. 2

    The Gamblification of Everything

    Tools originally designed to democratize finance—401(k)s, index funds, trading apps—repurposed as mechanisms of speculative extraction, turning citizens into compulsory gamblers on their own futures. Covers Robinhood's gamified interfaces, prediction markets, the normalization of sports betting, and the collapse of the line between investing and betting in financial journalism's own self-understanding.

  • Ch. 3

    The Scam Economy

    The explosion of outright fraud and the increasingly porous boundary between legal commerce and criminal enterprise. From industrial-scale scam call centers to Silicon Valley's "fake it till you make it" culture, the chapter argues that scams are not a bug in the grift economy but its most innovative sector—and that the FTC's finding of a 40% rise in scam payout rates is an index of a deeper epistemic damage.

  • Ch. 4

    The Transparent Con

    The grift economy's most distinctive political form: the leader who operates as an open con artist and gains support precisely because the con is visible. The tariff regime as extended case study—administered not according to economic logic but as an instrument of patronage where relief depends on political loyalty, effectively a state-run protection racket.

  • Ch. 5

    The Savvy and the Scammed

    What the grift economy does to subjectivity, trust, and the possibility of solidarity. The inversion of moral culpability—to be scammed is a reputational loss, evidence you took something at face value—and the recursive trap: we know we are being manipulated, pride ourselves on spotting the con, and then participate anyway, just more ironically. Critique becomes content; exposure deepens the logic.

  • Ch. 6

    After the Grift

    Refuses both nostalgia and despair. When the con is out in the open, the pretense of meritocracy collapses—and with it, the most powerful ideological defense the existing order ever had. The question is whether this visibility can be converted into democratic energy or will simply deepen cynicism.
