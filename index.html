Background

About

I'm an editor and writer who covers the intersection of economic power, financial markets, and everyday life — the places where a Fed decision or a tariff schedule or a corporate diversity rollback lands on real people with real bills.

For the past decade I've worked in financial and business journalism, most recently as a Senior Editor at Investopedia, where I led a team of ten, produced and edited hundreds of articles, and built more than 1,500 original data visualizations. Along the way I've been part of the conversation about what financial journalism looks like in the post-search era — how editors build audiences through discovery and social rather than keyword traffic, and what that means for how stories are told.

My background is unusual for financial journalism. Before moving into editorial work, I trained as a philosopher — my PhD focused on political theory and continental thought — and that formation shapes how I think about economic stories. I'm drawn to the structural over the symptomatic, the historical over the immediately reactive, the question that doesn't have an easy answer. It's why I spent months reporting on AI's effect on labor markets, tracing the argument back to earlier technology cycles. It's why a story about financial advisors and diversity became a long-form investigation into decades of industry failure rather than a round-up of opinion.

I'm currently at work on a book — Grift Society — examining how speculation and confidence became the organizing principles of contemporary American capitalism, from 401(k)s to Robinhood to prediction markets. The project sits at the intersection of financial journalism and critical theory, and it's been shaping how I think about money stories for years.

I'm based in New Orleans with my wife Jennifer, editor of Forbes Travel Guide. I'm looking for senior editorial roles — editing, writing, strategy — at publications where analytical depth and clear prose are treated as compatible rather than competing virtues.
